feature_set=['Longitude','Latitude']
X_matrix=train_rows.loc[:,feature_set]



for i in range(2,11):
    kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=i, random_state=0).fit(X_matrix)
    plt.figure(figsize=(8, 8))
    
    labels=set(kmeans.labels_)
    for k in labels:
        plt.scatter(X_matrix.iloc[kmeans.labels_ == k , 0] , X_matrix.iloc[kmeans.labels_ == k , 1] , label = k)
    plt.scatter(kmeans.cluster_centers_[:,0] , kmeans.cluster_centers_[:,1], s=60, color='b',label='Centroid')
    plt.xlabel(train_rows.columns[1])
    plt.ylabel(train_rows.columns[2])
    plt.title('Scatter plot for {} vs {}'.format (train_rows.columns[1],train_rows.columns[2]))
    plt.legend(bbox_to_anchor=(1.005,1.005), loc="upper left")
    plt.show()

Answer:¶

The sum of squared distance is used in the elbow method to choose an ideal value of k based on the distance between the sample points and their assigned clusters center. We will choose a value of k where we see an inflection point and the sum of squared distance begins to flatten out. When we are visualizing this graph, it looks somewhat like an elbow. The plot above shows that k = 3 is probably a good choice for the number of clusters. Also, from the above scatter plots we can see k=3 is a good choice, but k=4 and k=5 also seems like a good choice for the dataset. Based on both the elbow method and the scatter plots we can say that k=3 is a reasonable choice for the dataset.
